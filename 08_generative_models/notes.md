## 1. Why Generative Models Matter

All the models you have built so far — classifiers, regressors, sequence-to-sequence models — are *discriminative*. They learn $p(y \mid x)$: given an input, produce an output. A generative model learns $p(x)$ itself — the distribution of the data. Once you have $p(x)$, you can:

- **Sample** from it (create new images, text, audio that never existed)
- **Evaluate likelihood** (is this sample plausible under the data distribution?)
- **Infer latent structure** (what are the underlying factors of variation?)
- **Perform conditional generation** (generate an image given a text description)

The three dominant families of deep generative models are Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), and Diffusion Models. Each takes a fundamentally different approach to the same problem: learning to sample from a complex, high-dimensional distribution.

## 2. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

### 2.1 Intuition

A VAE wants to learn a compressed representation of the data (a "latent space") where nearby points decode to similar outputs, and every point decodes to *something* meaningful. It does this by forcing the encoder to output a probability distribution rather than a single point, and by regularizing those distributions to be close to a standard Gaussian.

Think of it this way: a regular autoencoder says "this image of a 3 lives at coordinates (1.2, 0.8) in latent space." A VAE says "this image of a 3 lives somewhere around (1.2, 0.8), with some uncertainty." By forcing that uncertainty to follow a standard normal distribution, we ensure the latent space is smooth and complete.

### 2.2 The Math: ELBO Derivation

This is the most important derivation in this module. Follow every step.

**Goal:** We want to maximize the log-likelihood of the data under our model:

$$\text{maximize} \quad \log p_\theta(x) \quad \text{for each training example } x$$

**Step 1 — Introduce the latent variable $z$.**

We assume each data point $x$ was generated by first sampling a latent code $z$ from a prior $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, then generating $x$ from a conditional distribution $p_\theta(x \mid z)$. The marginal likelihood is:

$$p_\theta(x) = \int p_\theta(x \mid z)\, p(z)\, dz$$

**Step 2 — Acknowledge the intractability.**

This integral requires evaluating $p_\theta(x \mid z)$ for every possible $z$ in a continuous, high-dimensional space. This is computationally intractable. We cannot evaluate $p_\theta(x)$ directly.

Similarly, the true posterior $p_\theta(z \mid x) = p_\theta(x \mid z)\, p(z)\, /\, p_\theta(x)$ is intractable because it requires $p_\theta(x)$ in the denominator.

**Step 3 — Introduce the variational approximation.**

Since we cannot compute $p_\theta(z \mid x)$, we introduce an approximation:

$$q_\phi(z \mid x) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(z;\; \mu_\phi(x),\; \text{diag}(\sigma_\phi^2(x))\right)$$

This is our encoder network, parameterized by $\phi$. Given an input $x$, it outputs a mean vector $\mu$ and a variance vector $\sigma^2$. We will optimize $\phi$ to make $q_\phi(z \mid x)$ as close to the true posterior $p_\theta(z \mid x)$ as possible.

**Step 4 — Derive the ELBO.**

Start from $\log p_\theta(x)$. We will show it equals the ELBO plus a non-negative gap.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\log p_\theta(x) &= \log p_\theta(x) \cdot 1 \\
&= \log p_\theta(x) \cdot \int q_\phi(z \mid x)\, dz & \text{[since } \int q = 1\text{]} \\
&= \int q_\phi(z \mid x) \cdot \log p_\theta(x)\, dz \\
&= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}[\log p_\theta(x)]
\end{aligned}
$$

Now use Bayes' rule: $p_\theta(x) = p_\theta(x, z)\, /\, p_\theta(z \mid x)$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
&= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\!\left[\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{p_\theta(z \mid x)}\right] \\
&= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\!\left[\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\right] + \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\!\left[\log \frac{q_\phi(z \mid x)}{p_\theta(z \mid x)}\right]
\end{aligned}
$$

The second term is $D_\text{KL}(q_\phi(z \mid x) \| p_\theta(z \mid x))$, which is always $\geq 0$.

Therefore:

$$\log p_\theta(x) = \text{ELBO} + D_\text{KL}(q_\phi(z \mid x) \| p_\theta(z \mid x))$$

$$\log p_\theta(x) \geq \text{ELBO}$$

where:

$$\text{ELBO} = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\!\left[\log \frac{p_\theta(x, z)}{q_\phi(z \mid x)}\right]$$

**Step 5 — Decompose the ELBO into interpretable terms.**

Expand $p_\theta(x, z) = p_\theta(x \mid z) \cdot p(z)$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{ELBO} &= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}[\log p_\theta(x \mid z) + \log p(z) - \log q_\phi(z \mid x)] \\
&= \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}[\log p_\theta(x \mid z)] - D_\text{KL}(q_\phi(z \mid x) \| p(z))
\end{aligned}
$$

**The final ELBO:**

$$\text{ELBO} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z \mid x)}[\log p_\theta(x \mid z)]}_{\text{Reconstruction term}} - \underbrace{D_\text{KL}(q_\phi(z \mid x) \| p(z))}_{\text{Regularization term}}$$

**Term 1 — Reconstruction:** "Sample $z$ from the encoder. How well does the decoder reconstruct $x$ from $z$?" This pushes the model to encode useful information. In practice, for continuous data this is the negative MSE loss; for binary data this is the binary cross-entropy.

**Term 2 — Regularization (KL divergence):** "How close is the encoder's output distribution to the prior $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$?" This pushes the encoder to produce distributions that overlap with the prior, ensuring the latent space is smooth and complete.

**Step 6 — Closed-form KL for Gaussians.**

When $q_\phi(z \mid x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu, \text{diag}(\sigma^2))$ and $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, the KL divergence has a closed-form solution:

$$D_\text{KL}(q \| p) = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_j \left(1 + \log \sigma_j^2 - \mu_j^2 - \sigma_j^2\right)$$

where $j$ indexes the dimensions of the latent space. This is easy to compute — no sampling needed.

### 2.3 The Reparameterization Trick

**The problem:** The reconstruction term requires sampling z ~ q_phi(z|x) = N(mu, sigma^2). But sampling is a stochastic operation — we cannot compute gradients through a random number generator. The encoder outputs mu and sigma, we sample z, and then we need to backpropagate through this sampling step to update mu and sigma.

**The solution:** Instead of sampling z ~ N(mu, sigma^2) directly, we reparameterize:

    epsilon ~ N(0, I)               [sample from a fixed distribution]
    z = mu + sigma * epsilon         [deterministic transformation]

Now z is a *deterministic function* of mu, sigma, and epsilon. The randomness is "externalized" into epsilon, which does not depend on the model parameters. Gradients with respect to mu and sigma flow through the deterministic computation:

    dz/d_mu = 1
    dz/d_sigma = epsilon

**Why this works:** The key insight is that sampling from N(mu, sigma^2) is equivalent to sampling from N(0, 1) and then scaling and shifting. By separating the randomness (epsilon) from the parameters (mu, sigma), we make the path from parameters to loss fully differentiable.

**In code:**

```python
def reparameterize(mu, logvar):
    """
    mu:     (batch_size, latent_dim) - encoder output mean
    logvar: (batch_size, latent_dim) - encoder output log-variance
    """
    std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar)   # sigma = exp(0.5 * log(sigma^2))
    eps = torch.randn_like(std)     # epsilon ~ N(0, I)
    z = mu + eps * std              # reparameterized sample
    return z
```

Note: The encoder outputs log(sigma^2) rather than sigma directly. This ensures numerical stability (sigma is always positive after exp) and avoids issues with very small variances.

### 2.4 Training Algorithm

```
Algorithm: VAE Training

Input: Dataset D, latent dimension d, learning rate eta
Initialize: encoder parameters phi, decoder parameters theta

For each epoch:
    For each minibatch x from D:
        1. Encode: mu, logvar = encoder_phi(x)
        2. Reparameterize: eps ~ N(0, I); z = mu + exp(0.5*logvar) * eps
        3. Decode: x_hat = decoder_theta(z)
        4. Compute reconstruction loss:
           L_recon = -sum(x * log(x_hat) + (1-x) * log(1-x_hat))  [BCE for binary]
           or L_recon = sum((x - x_hat)^2)                         [MSE for continuous]
        5. Compute KL divergence:
           L_KL = -0.5 * sum(1 + logvar - mu^2 - exp(logvar))
        6. Total loss: L = L_recon + beta * L_KL
        7. Backpropagate and update phi, theta
```

### 2.5 PyTorch Implementation

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class VAE(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_dim=784, hidden_dim=512, latent_dim=2):
        super().__init__()
        # Encoder
        self.enc_fc1 = nn.Linear(input_dim, hidden_dim)
        self.enc_fc2 = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim // 2)
        self.fc_mu = nn.Linear(hidden_dim // 2, latent_dim)
        self.fc_logvar = nn.Linear(hidden_dim // 2, latent_dim)
        # Decoder
        self.dec_fc1 = nn.Linear(latent_dim, hidden_dim // 2)
        self.dec_fc2 = nn.Linear(hidden_dim // 2, hidden_dim)
        self.dec_fc3 = nn.Linear(hidden_dim, input_dim)

    def encode(self, x):
        h = F.relu(self.enc_fc1(x))
        h = F.relu(self.enc_fc2(h))
        mu = self.fc_mu(h)
        logvar = self.fc_logvar(h)
        return mu, logvar

    def reparameterize(self, mu, logvar):
        std = torch.exp(0.5 * logvar)
        eps = torch.randn_like(std)
        return mu + eps * std

    def decode(self, z):
        h = F.relu(self.dec_fc1(z))
        h = F.relu(self.dec_fc2(h))
        return torch.sigmoid(self.dec_fc3(h))

    def forward(self, x):
        mu, logvar = self.encode(x.view(-1, 784))
        z = self.reparameterize(mu, logvar)
        x_recon = self.decode(z)
        return x_recon, mu, logvar


def vae_loss(x_recon, x, mu, logvar, beta=1.0):
    """
    Compute the VAE loss = reconstruction + beta * KL divergence.
    """
    # Reconstruction loss (binary cross-entropy, summed over features)
    recon_loss = F.binary_cross_entropy(x_recon, x.view(-1, 784), reduction='sum')
    # KL divergence: -0.5 * sum(1 + logvar - mu^2 - exp(logvar))
    kl_loss = -0.5 * torch.sum(1 + logvar - mu.pow(2) - logvar.exp())
    return recon_loss + beta * kl_loss


# Training loop
def train_vae(model, dataloader, optimizer, epochs=20, beta=1.0):
    model.train()
    for epoch in range(epochs):
        total_loss = 0
        for batch_x, _ in dataloader:
            x_recon, mu, logvar = model(batch_x)
            loss = vae_loss(x_recon, batch_x, mu, logvar, beta=beta)
            optimizer.zero_grad()
            loss.backward()
            optimizer.step()
            total_loss += loss.item()
        avg_loss = total_loss / len(dataloader.dataset)
        print(f"Epoch {epoch+1}/{epochs}, Loss: {avg_loss:.4f}")
```

### 2.6 Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Principled probabilistic framework with a well-defined objective (ELBO).
- Produces a structured, interpretable latent space.
- Can compute (a lower bound on) the likelihood of data.
- Training is stable — no adversarial dynamics.
- Latent space supports meaningful interpolation.

**Weaknesses:**
- Generated samples tend to be blurry, especially for complex data like faces.
- The Gaussian assumption for both the encoder and decoder is limiting.
- The KL term can cause "posterior collapse" — the encoder learns to ignore z and the decoder relies only on its own capacity.
- Sample quality is generally lower than GANs or diffusion models.

**When to use:** When you need a structured latent space (e.g., for representation learning, disentanglement, or data compression), when you need likelihood estimation, or when training stability is more important than sample sharpness.

## 3. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

### 3.1 Intuition

A GAN pits two networks against each other: a *generator* that creates fake samples and a *discriminator* that tries to distinguish real from fake. The generator gets better at fooling the discriminator, and the discriminator gets better at catching fakes. This adversarial competition drives both networks to improve, ideally until the generator produces samples indistinguishable from real data.

There is no explicit density estimation. The generator learns to transform noise into realistic samples through the pressure of the adversarial game alone.

### 3.2 The Math: GAN Objective

**The minimax formulation:**

    min_G max_D V(D, G) = E_{x ~ p_data}[log D(x)] + E_{z ~ p_z}[log(1 - D(G(z)))]

where:
- G: generator network, maps noise z to a sample G(z)
- D: discriminator network, maps a sample to a probability D(x) in [0, 1]
- p_data: the true data distribution
- p_z: the noise distribution (typically N(0, I) or Uniform)

**The discriminator's perspective (maximize V with respect to D):**

    L_D = E_{x ~ p_data}[log D(x)] + E_{z ~ p_z}[log(1 - D(G(z)))]

This is binary cross-entropy. The discriminator wants:
- D(x_real) -> 1 for real samples (first term maximized)
- D(G(z)) -> 0 for fake samples (second term maximized)

**The generator's perspective (minimize V with respect to G):**

    L_G = E_{z ~ p_z}[log(1 - D(G(z)))]

The generator wants D(G(z)) -> 1 (discriminator fooled).

**The practical generator loss:** In practice, minimizing log(1 - D(G(z))) has vanishing gradients when D(G(z)) is close to 0 (early in training, when G is bad). Instead, we maximize:

    L_G = E_{z ~ p_z}[log D(G(z))]

This provides stronger gradients early in training. It is sometimes called the "non-saturating" loss.

**Theoretical result (Goodfellow et al.):** For a fixed G, the optimal discriminator is:

    D*(x) = p_data(x) / (p_data(x) + p_G(x))

Substituting this back into the objective shows that the generator minimizes the Jensen-Shannon divergence between p_data and p_G. At the Nash equilibrium, p_G = p_data and D*(x) = 1/2 everywhere.

### 3.3 Training Dynamics and Instabilities

**Mode Collapse**

The generator discovers a small set of outputs that reliably fool the discriminator and produces only those. Instead of learning the full data distribution, it "collapses" to a few modes.

Concrete example on MNIST:
1. G learns to produce convincing "7"s. D is fooled.
2. D catches on: "everything looks like 7, must be fake." D learns to reject "7"-like images.
3. G switches to producing "3"s. D is fooled again.
4. D catches on again. G switches back to "7"s.
5. This oscillation continues. G never learns to produce all 10 digits simultaneously.

The root cause is that the minimax objective does not penalize lack of diversity. The generator is only measured by how well it fools the discriminator, not by how diverse its outputs are.

**Vanishing Gradients**

When the discriminator becomes too strong — D(G(z)) approaches 0 for all generated samples — the gradient of log(1 - D(G(z))) with respect to G's parameters vanishes. The generator receives no learning signal and stops improving.

Mathematically: d/dx log(1 - x) = -1/(1 - x). When x = D(G(z)) is near 0, the gradient is approximately -1, which is fine. But the gradient of log(x) when x is near 0 goes to negative infinity, which is why the non-saturating loss helps. However, even with the non-saturating loss, a perfect discriminator provides uninformative gradients because it classifies everything with extreme confidence.

**Training Oscillation**

GAN training is a two-player game, not a single optimization problem. Gradient descent on minimax objectives does not guarantee convergence. The two networks can chase each other indefinitely in parameter space without settling.

Symptoms: losses oscillate, sample quality fluctuates between good and bad, metrics are unreliable.

### 3.4 Wasserstein GAN (WGAN)

**Motivation:** The Jensen-Shannon divergence (used by the original GAN) is 0 or undefined when p_data and p_G have non-overlapping support. In high dimensions, this is almost always the case — real images live on a low-dimensional manifold, and so do generated images, and these manifolds almost surely don't overlap perfectly.

**The Wasserstein-1 distance (Earth Mover's distance):**

    W(p_data, p_G) = inf_{gamma in Pi(p_data, p_G)} E_{(x,y) ~ gamma}[||x - y||]

Intuitively: what is the minimum cost of transporting one pile of dirt (p_data) to match another (p_G), where cost = amount of dirt times distance moved?

**Key property:** W(p_data, p_G) is continuous and differentiable almost everywhere, even when the distributions don't overlap. It provides a meaningful gradient signal that the JS divergence does not.

**Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality:**

    W(p_data, p_G) = sup_{||f||_L <= 1} E_{x ~ p_data}[f(x)] - E_{x ~ p_G}[f(x)]

where the supremum is over all 1-Lipschitz functions f (functions where |f(a) - f(b)| <= |a - b| for all a, b).

**The WGAN objective:**

    min_G max_{D: ||D||_L <= 1} E_{x ~ p_data}[D(x)] - E_{z ~ p_z}[D(G(z))]

Note: no log, no sigmoid on D's output. D (now called the "critic") outputs a real number, not a probability.

**Enforcing the Lipschitz constraint:**

Original WGAN: clip weights of D to [-c, c]. This is crude and leads to either vanishing or exploding gradients depending on c.

**WGAN-GP (Gradient Penalty):** Instead of clipping, add a penalty that encourages the gradient norm of D to be close to 1:

    L_D = E_{z ~ p_z}[D(G(z))] - E_{x ~ p_data}[D(x)] + lambda * E_{x_hat ~ p_{x_hat}}[(||grad_{x_hat} D(x_hat)||_2 - 1)^2]

where x_hat = alpha * x_real + (1 - alpha) * x_fake, with alpha ~ Uniform(0, 1). The gradient penalty is evaluated on random interpolations between real and fake samples.

lambda = 10 is the standard value. This works much better than weight clipping.

### 3.5 Training Algorithm

```
Algorithm: GAN Training (Standard)

Input: Dataset D, latent dimension d, learning rate eta, n_critic steps
Initialize: generator params theta_G, discriminator params theta_D

For each epoch:
    For each minibatch x_real from D:

        # --- Train Discriminator (n_critic steps) ---
        For k = 1 to n_critic:
            Sample z ~ N(0, I), shape (batch_size, d)
            x_fake = G(z)                        [detach from G's graph]
            L_D = -mean(log(D(x_real))) - mean(log(1 - D(x_fake)))
            Update theta_D to minimize L_D

        # --- Train Generator (1 step) ---
        Sample z ~ N(0, I), shape (batch_size, d)
        x_fake = G(z)
        L_G = -mean(log(D(x_fake)))             [non-saturating loss]
        Update theta_G to minimize L_G
```

```
Algorithm: WGAN-GP Training

For each epoch:
    For each minibatch x_real from D:

        # --- Train Critic (n_critic = 5 steps) ---
        For k = 1 to 5:
            Sample z ~ N(0, I)
            x_fake = G(z).detach()
            alpha = Uniform(0, 1), shape (batch_size, 1, 1, 1)
            x_hat = alpha * x_real + (1 - alpha) * x_fake
            x_hat.requires_grad_(True)

            critic_real = D(x_real).mean()
            critic_fake = D(x_fake).mean()
            grad = autograd.grad(D(x_hat).sum(), x_hat, create_graph=True)[0]
            grad_penalty = ((grad.norm(2, dim=1) - 1) ** 2).mean()

            L_D = critic_fake - critic_real + 10 * grad_penalty
            Update theta_D to minimize L_D

        # --- Train Generator ---
        Sample z ~ N(0, I)
        L_G = -D(G(z)).mean()
        Update theta_G to minimize L_G
```

### 3.6 PyTorch Implementation (DCGAN Components)

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn


class DCGANGenerator(nn.Module):
    """
    DCGAN Generator for 64x64 images.
    Input: latent vector z of shape (batch_size, latent_dim, 1, 1)
    Output: image of shape (batch_size, 3, 64, 64)
    """
    def __init__(self, latent_dim=100, feature_maps=64, channels=3):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            # Input: (latent_dim, 1, 1) -> (feature_maps*8, 4, 4)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(latent_dim, feature_maps * 8, 4, 1, 0, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feature_maps * 8),
            nn.ReLU(True),
            # (feature_maps*8, 4, 4) -> (feature_maps*4, 8, 8)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(feature_maps * 8, feature_maps * 4, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feature_maps * 4),
            nn.ReLU(True),
            # (feature_maps*4, 8, 8) -> (feature_maps*2, 16, 16)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(feature_maps * 4, feature_maps * 2, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feature_maps * 2),
            nn.ReLU(True),
            # (feature_maps*2, 16, 16) -> (feature_maps, 32, 32)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(feature_maps * 2, feature_maps, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feature_maps),
            nn.ReLU(True),
            # (feature_maps, 32, 32) -> (channels, 64, 64)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(feature_maps, channels, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.Tanh(),
        )

    def forward(self, z):
        return self.net(z)


class DCGANDiscriminator(nn.Module):
    """
    DCGAN Discriminator for 64x64 images.
    Input: image of shape (batch_size, 3, 64, 64)
    Output: probability (batch_size, 1, 1, 1)
    """
    def __init__(self, feature_maps=64, channels=3):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            # (channels, 64, 64) -> (feature_maps, 32, 32)
            nn.Conv2d(channels, feature_maps, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # (feature_maps, 32, 32) -> (feature_maps*2, 16, 16)
            nn.Conv2d(feature_maps, feature_maps * 2, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feature_maps * 2),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # (feature_maps*2, 16, 16) -> (feature_maps*4, 8, 8)
            nn.Conv2d(feature_maps * 2, feature_maps * 4, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feature_maps * 4),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # (feature_maps*4, 8, 8) -> (feature_maps*8, 4, 4)
            nn.Conv2d(feature_maps * 4, feature_maps * 8, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feature_maps * 8),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # (feature_maps*8, 4, 4) -> (1, 1, 1)
            nn.Conv2d(feature_maps * 8, 1, 4, 1, 0, bias=False),
            nn.Sigmoid(),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.net(x)


def compute_gradient_penalty(discriminator, real_samples, fake_samples, device):
    """Compute gradient penalty for WGAN-GP."""
    alpha = torch.rand(real_samples.size(0), 1, 1, 1, device=device)
    interpolates = (alpha * real_samples + (1 - alpha) * fake_samples).requires_grad_(True)
    d_interpolates = discriminator(interpolates)
    gradients = torch.autograd.grad(
        outputs=d_interpolates,
        inputs=interpolates,
        grad_outputs=torch.ones_like(d_interpolates),
        create_graph=True,
        retain_graph=True,
    )[0]
    gradients = gradients.view(gradients.size(0), -1)
    gradient_penalty = ((gradients.norm(2, dim=1) - 1) ** 2).mean()
    return gradient_penalty
```

### 3.7 Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Produces sharp, high-quality samples (especially with architectural advances like StyleGAN).
- No explicit density estimation needed — learns to sample directly.
- Flexible: can condition on almost anything (class labels, text, images).
- Conceptually elegant: the adversarial game is a powerful inductive bias.

**Weaknesses:**
- Training is notoriously unstable (mode collapse, oscillation, vanishing gradients).
- No likelihood computation — you cannot evaluate p(x) for a given sample.
- Mode collapse: the generator may not cover the full data distribution.
- Evaluation is difficult (FID and IS are imperfect proxies).
- Requires careful hyperparameter tuning and architectural choices.
- Two-timescale training (D and G learning rates, n_critic steps) adds complexity.

**When to use:** When sample quality is the primary concern and you don't need likelihood estimation. When you have the expertise to manage training instabilities. When you need high-resolution, photorealistic generation.

## 4. Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs)

### 4.1 Intuition

A diffusion model learns to reverse a gradual noising process. The forward process takes a clean image and adds a tiny bit of Gaussian noise at each step, repeating for T steps until the image is pure noise. The reverse process — which is what the neural network learns — starts from pure noise and removes noise step by step, ultimately producing a clean image.

The beauty is in the decomposition: instead of learning to generate an image in one shot (hard), we learn to denoise an image by one small step (much easier). Chain together T small denoising steps, and you get a full generative model.

### 4.2 The Math: The Forward Process

**Definition:** Given a clean data point x_0, the forward process produces a sequence x_1, x_2, ..., x_T by adding Gaussian noise:

    q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = N(x_t; sqrt(1 - beta_t) * x_{t-1}, beta_t * I)

where beta_1, beta_2, ..., beta_T is the noise schedule (typically beta_1 = 0.0001, beta_T = 0.02, with T = 1000).

**What each step does:**
- Shrink the signal by a factor of sqrt(1 - beta_t) (slightly less than 1)
- Add Gaussian noise with variance beta_t (small)

After many steps, the signal is destroyed and x_T is approximately N(0, I).

**The closed-form for arbitrary timestep t:**

Define:
- alpha_t = 1 - beta_t
- alpha_bar_t = alpha_1 * alpha_2 * ... * alpha_t = product_{s=1}^{t} alpha_s

Then:

    q(x_t | x_0) = N(x_t; sqrt(alpha_bar_t) * x_0, (1 - alpha_bar_t) * I)

Which means we can sample x_t directly:

    x_t = sqrt(alpha_bar_t) * x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon,    epsilon ~ N(0, I)

**Derivation of the closed form:**

Step 1: x_1 = sqrt(alpha_1) * x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_1) * epsilon_1

Step 2: x_2 = sqrt(alpha_2) * x_1 + sqrt(1 - alpha_2) * epsilon_2
             = sqrt(alpha_2) * (sqrt(alpha_1) * x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_1) * epsilon_1) + sqrt(1 - alpha_2) * epsilon_2
             = sqrt(alpha_1 * alpha_2) * x_0 + sqrt(alpha_2(1 - alpha_1)) * epsilon_1 + sqrt(1 - alpha_2) * epsilon_2

The sum of two independent Gaussians N(0, alpha_2(1-alpha_1)) and N(0, 1-alpha_2) is N(0, alpha_2 - alpha_1*alpha_2 + 1 - alpha_2) = N(0, 1 - alpha_1*alpha_2) = N(0, 1 - alpha_bar_2).

So: x_2 = sqrt(alpha_bar_2) * x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_2) * epsilon, where epsilon ~ N(0, I).

By induction, this holds for any t.

**The noise schedule matters:**
- Linear schedule: beta_t increases linearly from beta_1 to beta_T. Simple, used in the original DDPM.
- Cosine schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021): alpha_bar_t follows a cosine curve. This prevents too-rapid noise addition in later steps, improving sample quality.

### 4.3 The Math: The Reverse Process

**Goal:** Learn p_theta(x_{t-1} | x_t) — how to remove a little noise from x_t to get x_{t-1}.

**Parameterization:** We model the reverse step as Gaussian:

    p_theta(x_{t-1} | x_t) = N(x_{t-1}; mu_theta(x_t, t), sigma_t^2 * I)

The neural network must output the mean mu_theta. The variance sigma_t^2 is typically set to beta_t (fixed).

**The noise prediction reformulation:**

It turns out that instead of predicting the mean mu_theta directly, it is better to predict the noise epsilon that was added. The mean can then be recovered:

    mu_theta(x_t, t) = 1/sqrt(alpha_t) * (x_t - beta_t/sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon_theta(x_t, t))

where epsilon_theta is the neural network.

**Derivation of this formula:**

We know x_t = sqrt(alpha_bar_t) * x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon. Solving for x_0:

    x_0 = (x_t - sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon) / sqrt(alpha_bar_t)

The true reverse process posterior (when conditioned on x_0) is:

    q(x_{t-1} | x_t, x_0) = N(x_{t-1}; mu_tilde_t, beta_tilde_t * I)

where:

    mu_tilde_t = sqrt(alpha_bar_{t-1}) * beta_t / (1 - alpha_bar_t) * x_0
               + sqrt(alpha_t) * (1 - alpha_bar_{t-1}) / (1 - alpha_bar_t) * x_t

Substituting our expression for x_0 in terms of epsilon:

    mu_tilde_t = 1/sqrt(alpha_t) * (x_t - beta_t / sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon)

So predicting epsilon is equivalent to predicting the mean of the reverse process.

### 4.4 The Math: Training Objective

**The variational bound:** Like VAEs, we can derive a variational lower bound on log p_theta(x_0):

    log p_theta(x_0) >= E_q[-log p(x_T) - sum_{t=1}^{T} log(p_theta(x_{t-1}|x_t) / q(x_t|x_{t-1}))]

This decomposes into T KL divergence terms, one for each timestep.

**The simplified objective (Ho et al., 2020):**

After considerable algebra (dropping terms that don't depend on theta and simplifying), the objective reduces to:

    L_simple = E_{t ~ Uniform(1,T), x_0 ~ p_data, epsilon ~ N(0,I)} [|| epsilon - epsilon_theta(x_t, t) ||^2]

where x_t = sqrt(alpha_bar_t) * x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon.

**In words:** Sample a training image x_0. Pick a random timestep t. Compute the noisy version x_t. Ask the neural network to predict what noise was added. Minimize the mean squared error between the predicted noise and the actual noise.

This is remarkably simple. The entire diffusion model training loop is:
1. Sample x_0 from the dataset
2. Sample t uniformly from {1, 2, ..., T}
3. Sample epsilon ~ N(0, I)
4. Compute x_t = sqrt(alpha_bar_t) * x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon
5. Compute loss = || epsilon - epsilon_theta(x_t, t) ||^2
6. Backpropagate and update theta

### 4.5 The Connection to Score Matching

The "score" of a distribution p(x) is the gradient of its log-density:

    s(x) = grad_x log p(x)

Score matching trains a neural network s_theta(x) to approximate this gradient. The key insight (Song & Ermon, 2019) is that:

    epsilon_theta(x_t, t) is proportional to -sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * s_theta(x_t, t)

Predicting the noise is equivalent (up to a scaling factor) to predicting the score. This connection unifies DDPM with the score-based generative modeling framework and provides deeper theoretical understanding.

### 4.6 Walkthrough: One Denoising Step

Let us trace through one step of sampling to make the shapes and operations concrete. Assume we are generating 28x28 grayscale images (MNIST), batch size B=16, T=1000.

**Setup:**
- x_t: shape (16, 1, 28, 28) — the current noisy image
- t: the current timestep (a scalar, say t=500)
- epsilon_theta: U-Net that takes (x_t, t) and outputs predicted noise of shape (16, 1, 28, 28)

**Step-by-step:**

```
1. Embed the timestep:
   t_emb = sinusoidal_embedding(t)     # shape: (16, emb_dim)
   This tells the network the current noise level.

2. Predict the noise:
   epsilon_hat = unet(x_t, t_emb)      # shape: (16, 1, 28, 28)

3. Compute the predicted mean of x_{t-1}:
   mu = (1 / sqrt(alpha_t)) * (x_t - (beta_t / sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t)) * epsilon_hat)
   # shape: (16, 1, 28, 28)
   # alpha_t, beta_t, alpha_bar_t are scalars looked up from precomputed schedules

4. Sample noise (unless t = 1):
   z = torch.randn_like(x_t) if t > 1 else torch.zeros_like(x_t)
   # shape: (16, 1, 28, 28)

5. Compute x_{t-1}:
   sigma_t = sqrt(beta_t)              # scalar
   x_{t-1} = mu + sigma_t * z          # shape: (16, 1, 28, 28)
```

That is one step. Repeat 1000 times (from t=T down to t=1) to generate x_0.

### 4.7 Classifier-Free Guidance

**Problem:** Conditional generation (e.g., "generate an image of a cat") often produces samples that are too diverse and not sufficiently aligned with the condition.

**Solution (Ho & Salimans, 2022):** During training, randomly drop the conditioning signal (replace it with a null token) with some probability (e.g., 10%). This trains the same model to handle both conditional and unconditional generation.

At inference time, compute both predictions and extrapolate:

    epsilon_guided = epsilon_theta(x_t, t, null) + w * (epsilon_theta(x_t, t, c) - epsilon_theta(x_t, t, null))

where:
- c is the conditioning signal (e.g., class label or text embedding)
- null is the unconditional input
- w is the guidance scale (w > 1 amplifies conditioning; w = 1 is standard conditional; w = 0 is unconditional)

**Why it works:** The difference (epsilon_conditional - epsilon_unconditional) represents the "direction" in noise-prediction space that the conditioning signal points toward. Multiplying by w > 1 moves further in that direction, making the output more strongly aligned with the condition at the cost of some diversity.

Typical values: w = 7.5 for text-to-image (Stable Diffusion).

### 4.8 The U-Net Backbone

The denoising network epsilon_theta is almost always a U-Net architecture with the following components:

**Encoder path:** A series of downsampling blocks, each containing:
- Convolutional layers (typically ResNet blocks)
- Timestep embedding injection (added to feature maps)
- Self-attention at lower resolutions (e.g., 16x16, 8x8)
- Downsampling via strided convolution

**Bottleneck:** Self-attention + ResNet blocks at the lowest resolution.

**Decoder path:** A series of upsampling blocks, each containing:
- Convolutional layers (ResNet blocks)
- Timestep embedding injection
- Cross-attention with conditioning signal (for conditional models)
- Skip connections from the encoder (concatenated, not added)
- Upsampling via transposed convolution or nearest-neighbor + conv

**Timestep conditioning:** The timestep t is embedded using sinusoidal positional encoding (same idea as in Transformers), projected through an MLP, and added to the feature maps at each layer. This tells the network the current noise level.

### 4.9 Latent Diffusion (Stable Diffusion)

**Problem:** Running diffusion in pixel space at high resolution (e.g., 512x512x3) is extremely expensive. Each denoising step requires a full forward pass of the U-Net, and there are typically 50-1000 steps.

**Solution (Rombach et al., 2022):**
1. Train a VAE (or VQ-VAE) to encode images into a lower-dimensional latent space: z = Encode(x), where x is 512x512x3 and z might be 64x64x4.
2. Run the entire diffusion process in this latent space, not in pixel space.
3. After sampling z_0 from the diffusion model, decode to pixel space: x = Decode(z_0).

**Benefits:**
- The latent space is ~48x smaller than pixel space (in the Stable Diffusion case).
- Training and sampling are dramatically faster.
- The VAE captures perceptually irrelevant detail, letting the diffusion model focus on semantic content.

**Cross-attention for text conditioning:** In Stable Diffusion, the text prompt is encoded by a text encoder (CLIP). At each attention layer in the U-Net, cross-attention is computed between the image features (queries) and text features (keys and values). This is how the model "understands" the text prompt.

### 4.10 Training Algorithm

```
Algorithm: DDPM Training

Input: Dataset D, T timesteps, noise schedule beta_1...beta_T
Precompute: alpha_t = 1 - beta_t, alpha_bar_t = prod(alpha_1...alpha_t)
Initialize: noise prediction network epsilon_theta

Repeat until convergence:
    1. Sample x_0 from D                           # a clean training image
    2. Sample t ~ Uniform({1, 2, ..., T})           # a random timestep
    3. Sample epsilon ~ N(0, I)                      # the noise to add
    4. Compute x_t = sqrt(alpha_bar_t) * x_0 + sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t) * epsilon
    5. Predict noise: epsilon_hat = epsilon_theta(x_t, t)
    6. Compute loss: L = || epsilon - epsilon_hat ||^2
    7. Backpropagate and update theta
```

```
Algorithm: DDPM Sampling

Input: Trained model epsilon_theta, T timesteps, noise schedule
Precompute: alpha_t, alpha_bar_t, beta_t, sigma_t = sqrt(beta_t)

1. Sample x_T ~ N(0, I)
2. For t = T, T-1, ..., 1:
   a. epsilon_hat = epsilon_theta(x_t, t)
   b. mu = (1/sqrt(alpha_t)) * (x_t - (beta_t / sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t)) * epsilon_hat)
   c. If t > 1: z ~ N(0, I), else: z = 0
   d. x_{t-1} = mu + sigma_t * z
3. Return x_0
```

### 4.11 PyTorch Implementation (Core Components)

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
import math


class SinusoidalPositionEmbedding(nn.Module):
    """Encode timestep t into a vector using sinusoidal embeddings."""
    def __init__(self, dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.dim = dim

    def forward(self, t):
        device = t.device
        half_dim = self.dim // 2
        emb = math.log(10000) / (half_dim - 1)
        emb = torch.exp(torch.arange(half_dim, device=device) * -emb)
        emb = t[:, None].float() * emb[None, :]
        emb = torch.cat([emb.sin(), emb.cos()], dim=-1)
        return emb


class ResidualBlock(nn.Module):
    """A residual block with timestep conditioning."""
    def __init__(self, in_ch, out_ch, time_emb_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_ch, out_ch, 3, padding=1)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(out_ch, out_ch, 3, padding=1)
        self.time_mlp = nn.Linear(time_emb_dim, out_ch)
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_ch)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_ch)
        self.residual_conv = nn.Conv2d(in_ch, out_ch, 1) if in_ch != out_ch else nn.Identity()

    def forward(self, x, t_emb):
        h = F.relu(self.bn1(self.conv1(x)))
        # Add timestep embedding
        h = h + self.time_mlp(t_emb)[:, :, None, None]
        h = F.relu(self.bn2(self.conv2(h)))
        return h + self.residual_conv(x)


class SimpleUNet(nn.Module):
    """
    A simplified U-Net for diffusion on 28x28 images.
    Not production-grade, but captures the essential structure.
    """
    def __init__(self, in_channels=1, time_emb_dim=128):
        super().__init__()
        self.time_embed = nn.Sequential(
            SinusoidalPositionEmbedding(time_emb_dim),
            nn.Linear(time_emb_dim, time_emb_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
        )
        # Encoder
        self.down1 = ResidualBlock(in_channels, 64, time_emb_dim)
        self.down2 = ResidualBlock(64, 128, time_emb_dim)
        self.pool = nn.MaxPool2d(2)
        # Bottleneck
        self.bottleneck = ResidualBlock(128, 256, time_emb_dim)
        # Decoder
        self.up2 = nn.ConvTranspose2d(256, 128, 2, stride=2)
        self.dec2 = ResidualBlock(256, 128, time_emb_dim)  # 256 because of skip connection
        self.up1 = nn.ConvTranspose2d(128, 64, 2, stride=2)
        self.dec1 = ResidualBlock(128, 64, time_emb_dim)   # 128 because of skip connection
        # Output
        self.out_conv = nn.Conv2d(64, in_channels, 1)

    def forward(self, x, t):
        t_emb = self.time_embed(t)
        # Encoder
        d1 = self.down1(x, t_emb)                # (B, 64, 28, 28)
        d2 = self.down2(self.pool(d1), t_emb)     # (B, 128, 14, 14)
        # Bottleneck
        b = self.bottleneck(self.pool(d2), t_emb)  # (B, 256, 7, 7)
        # Decoder with skip connections
        u2 = self.up2(b)                           # (B, 128, 14, 14)
        u2 = self.dec2(torch.cat([u2, d2], dim=1), t_emb)  # (B, 128, 14, 14)
        u1 = self.up1(u2)                          # (B, 64, 28, 28)
        u1 = self.dec1(torch.cat([u1, d1], dim=1), t_emb)  # (B, 64, 28, 28)
        return self.out_conv(u1)                   # (B, 1, 28, 28)


class DiffusionModel:
    """Wraps the noise schedule and training/sampling logic."""

    def __init__(self, model, T=1000, beta_start=1e-4, beta_end=0.02, device='cpu'):
        self.model = model
        self.T = T
        self.device = device

        # Linear noise schedule
        self.betas = torch.linspace(beta_start, beta_end, T).to(device)
        self.alphas = 1.0 - self.betas
        self.alpha_bars = torch.cumprod(self.alphas, dim=0)

        # Precompute useful quantities
        self.sqrt_alpha_bars = torch.sqrt(self.alpha_bars)
        self.sqrt_one_minus_alpha_bars = torch.sqrt(1.0 - self.alpha_bars)
        self.sqrt_alphas = torch.sqrt(self.alphas)

    def forward_process(self, x_0, t):
        """Add noise to x_0 at timestep t. Returns (x_t, epsilon)."""
        epsilon = torch.randn_like(x_0)
        sqrt_ab = self.sqrt_alpha_bars[t][:, None, None, None]
        sqrt_1_ab = self.sqrt_one_minus_alpha_bars[t][:, None, None, None]
        x_t = sqrt_ab * x_0 + sqrt_1_ab * epsilon
        return x_t, epsilon

    def train_step(self, x_0, optimizer):
        """One training step: add noise, predict it, compute MSE loss."""
        batch_size = x_0.shape[0]
        t = torch.randint(0, self.T, (batch_size,), device=self.device)
        x_t, epsilon = self.forward_process(x_0, t)
        epsilon_pred = self.model(x_t, t)
        loss = F.mse_loss(epsilon_pred, epsilon)
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()
        return loss.item()

    @torch.no_grad()
    def sample(self, shape):
        """Generate samples by iterative denoising from pure noise."""
        x = torch.randn(shape, device=self.device)
        for t in reversed(range(self.T)):
            t_batch = torch.full((shape[0],), t, device=self.device, dtype=torch.long)
            epsilon_pred = self.model(x, t_batch)
            alpha_t = self.alphas[t]
            alpha_bar_t = self.alpha_bars[t]
            beta_t = self.betas[t]
            # Compute mean
            mu = (1 / torch.sqrt(alpha_t)) * (
                x - (beta_t / torch.sqrt(1 - alpha_bar_t)) * epsilon_pred
            )
            # Add noise (except at t=0)
            if t > 0:
                sigma = torch.sqrt(beta_t)
                z = torch.randn_like(x)
                x = mu + sigma * z
            else:
                x = mu
        return x
```

### 4.12 Why Diffusion Models Overtook GANs

By 2022, diffusion models had surpassed GANs on FID scores for ImageNet generation. Here is why:

1. **Training stability:** Diffusion models optimize a simple MSE loss. No adversarial dynamics, no mode collapse, no balancing two networks. Training just works.

2. **Mode coverage:** Because the objective is based on a likelihood bound, diffusion models naturally cover all modes of the data distribution. GANs notoriously drop modes.

3. **Scalability:** The U-Net + diffusion framework scales well with compute. Larger models and more data consistently improve results — a property that GANs did not reliably exhibit.

4. **Conditioning flexibility:** Classifier-free guidance made conditional generation dramatically better. Text-to-image diffusion models (DALL-E 2, Imagen, Stable Diffusion) far surpassed conditional GANs.

5. **Theoretical grounding:** The connection to score matching and variational inference gives diffusion models a principled probabilistic foundation. GANs lack this.

**The remaining weakness:** Sampling speed. Generating one image requires 50-1000 forward passes (one per denoising step). GANs need only one. This has driven research into faster samplers (DDIM, consistency models, distillation) that reduce sampling to 1-4 steps.

### 4.13 Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- State-of-the-art sample quality across many domains (images, audio, video, 3D).
- Training is simple and stable (MSE loss, no adversarial dynamics).
- Excellent mode coverage — captures the full diversity of the data distribution.
- Well-grounded in probability theory (variational inference, score matching).
- Flexible conditioning via classifier-free guidance.
- Computes a likelihood bound.

**Weaknesses:**
- Slow sampling: 50-1000 forward passes per sample (vs. 1 for GANs, 1 for VAEs).
- High computational cost for training (many timesteps, large U-Nets).
- Requires careful noise schedule design.
- The iterative sampling makes real-time applications challenging without distillation.

**When to use:** When you need the highest quality generation and mode coverage, when training stability matters, when you can afford the sampling cost (or use accelerated samplers), and in most modern generative modeling applications.

## 5. Comparison: VAE vs GAN vs Diffusion

### 5.1 Summary Table

| Property | VAE | GAN | Diffusion |
|----------|-----|-----|-----------|
| **Training stability** | Stable (single loss) | Unstable (adversarial) | Stable (MSE loss) |
| **Sample quality** | Blurry | Sharp | State-of-the-art |
| **Mode coverage** | Good | Poor (mode collapse) | Excellent |
| **Likelihood computation** | Lower bound (ELBO) | None | Lower bound (VLB) |
| **Sampling speed** | Fast (single pass) | Fast (single pass) | Slow (50-1000 steps) |
| **Training objective** | ELBO maximization | Minimax game | Noise prediction (MSE) |
| **Latent space** | Structured, smooth | No explicit latent structure | Implicit (noise schedule) |
| **Conditioning** | Straightforward | Requires care | Classifier-free guidance |
| **Diversity** | High | Variable (mode collapse risk) | High |
| **Computational cost** | Low | Medium | High |
| **Year of breakthrough** | 2013 | 2014 | 2020 |
| **Best use case** | Representation learning, compression | When speed + quality needed | Highest quality generation |

### 5.2 Choosing the Right Model

**Choose a VAE when:**
- You need a structured latent space for downstream tasks (interpolation, clustering, disentanglement).
- Training stability and simplicity are priorities.
- You need likelihood estimation.
- Slightly blurry samples are acceptable.
- You are working with limited compute.

**Choose a GAN when:**
- Sample quality and sharpness are paramount and you have GAN training expertise.
- You need fast single-pass generation.
- You don't need likelihood computation or mode coverage guarantees.
- Your domain has well-tested GAN architectures (e.g., face generation with StyleGAN).

**Choose a Diffusion Model when:**
- You need the highest possible sample quality and diversity.
- Training stability is important and you want to avoid adversarial training pitfalls.
- You can tolerate slower sampling (or use accelerated methods like DDIM or consistency models).
- You need flexible conditioning (text-to-image, inpainting, super-resolution).
- You are starting a new generative modeling project in 2024+ (diffusion is the default choice for most applications).

### 5.3 Hybrid Approaches

Modern systems often combine ideas from multiple families:

- **Latent Diffusion (Stable Diffusion):** Uses a VAE encoder to compress images into a latent space, then runs diffusion in that space. Combines VAE's efficient representation with diffusion's generation quality.
- **VQ-VAE + Autoregressive:** Uses a VQ-VAE to discretize the latent space, then generates latent codes autoregressively. Combines VAE's structure with transformer generation.
- **Consistency Models:** Distill a trained diffusion model into a single-step generator, approaching GAN-like speed with diffusion-like quality.

The boundaries between these families are blurring. Understanding all three is essential because modern architectures freely combine their ideas.

### 6.2 Key Insight

Each generative model family solved the problem its predecessor could not:

- **VAEs** gave us principled probabilistic generation but blurry samples.
- **GANs** gave us sharp samples but unstable training and mode collapse.
- **Diffusion models** gave us sharp, diverse samples with stable training — but slow sampling.

The field continues to iterate. Flow matching and consistency models aim to combine the quality of diffusion with the speed of GANs. Understanding the trajectory — why each approach was invented, what problem it solved, and what limitation it introduced — gives you the context to understand where the field is heading next.
